## What is Infinity

Infinity is a 28mm sci-fi skirmish game by [Corvus Belli](/studios/corvus-belli/), set in the Human Sphere: a near-future where humanity has spread across the stars, fractured into rival hyper-powers, and is currently being invaded by an alien empire called the Combined Army. Each player runs a small force of 8 to 15 highly-specialized models drawn from one of eight major factions and dozens of sub-factional "sectorials."

The game is built around two ideas that you will not find at the same intensity in any other major skirmish game:

**The order pool.** Every model on the table contributes one Order to a shared pool at the start of your turn. On your active turn you spend those orders to activate models. You can pour every order into one specialist and run them across the board, you can spread orders across the whole force, or anything in between. Lieutenants, Hackers, and certain specialists can generate extra orders or steal them from the opponent. The order economy *is* the game: you are always paying for the activation you actually want.

**ARO (Automatic Reaction Order).** When an active model does something in line of sight of an enemy, that enemy gets to respond in real time. Shoot back, dodge, hack, throw smoke, change facing, declare a long skill. Almost every meaningful action on your active turn is answered, immediately, by the opponent. Movement is a tactical question, not a free phase. The reactive player is never bored, because the reactive player is making decisions on almost every order their opponent spends. This single mechanic is what makes Infinity feel different the first time you play it.

On top of those two pillars sit the things you'd recognize from any modern miniatures game: cover, suppression fire, line of sight (which is *actually* line of sight here, drawn from the model's perspective through 3D terrain), camouflage and stealth states, hacking, smoke, airborne deployment, doctor and engineer specialists, and a deep set of skills and equipment that let two armies of similar points score points feel completely different to play.

The first edition shipped in 2005. The current edition is **N5**, the fifth major revision, which streamlined and rebalanced large parts of N4 while keeping the order pool and ARO at the heart of the system. Standard tournament play (ITS, the Infinity Tournament System) is **300 points**, played on a heavily-terrained 4 × 4 ft table over typically four turns.

## Why play it

Infinity is the skirmish game for people who want every decision to count. Lists are small enough that you actually know what every model on the table does, but the order pool means even a 10-model list generates a turn full of branching tactical choices: which model burns the orders, which specialist you commit, when you spend a Lieutenant order, when you trigger your Coordinated Order to crash the line. There is no autopilot turn. There is no "I move my whole army six inches and pass priority." Every order is paid for.

The reactive turn is the part that hooks people. Most miniatures games have a polite back-and-forth: I do my turn, you do yours, and the opponent is mostly a spectator while you push your models around. In Infinity, your opponent is *with you* on your active turn, ARO after ARO, deciding whether to shoot you, dodge you, or save the reaction for the next model coming around the corner. It is the most interactive miniatures game I have ever played and probably the closest a tabletop wargame gets to feeling like a real-time strategy game.

The setting carries a lot of weight too. The Human Sphere is a high-concept sci-fi world: hyper-corporations, religious states, a recovering lost colony, a pan-Asian space empire, anarchist motherships, an actual benevolent superintelligent AI, and an invading alien hegemony built around an Evolved Intelligence and the species it absorbs. The miniatures are some of the best in the industry. The aesthetic is anime-influenced sci-fi (heavily Ghost in the Shell, Appleseed, and Masamune Shirow generally) without ever being on-the-nose about it.

The other thing that gets understated: **the rules are free.** Every rule in Infinity is on Corvus Belli's [official resources page](https://infinityuniverse.com/resources), and [Infinity Army](https://infinityuniverse.com/army/infinity) is a fully-featured online list builder that's also free. You can learn the game and build a working list before you buy a single model.

## The factions

Infinity is unusual in how distinctly each faction plays. Each one has a setting identity, a model aesthetic, and a tactical profile that holds up across sectorials. The current factions in N5:

- **PanOceania.** The dominant hyper-power of the Human Sphere. High-tech, well-funded, religious-tinted, often the "shooty heavy infantry with the best toys" faction. The TAGs (giant mechs) are iconic.
- **Yu Jing.** The pan-Asian StateEmpire, second superpower of the Sphere. Aesthetically mecha-samurai, tactically flexible, with strong heavy infantry and a great mix of specialists.
- **Ariadna.** Descendants of a lost colony ship that crashed on Dawn. Lower-tech, mostly camo and infantry, with an in-universe excuse to have French legionnaires, Caledonian highlanders, Cossacks, and U.S. Rangers in the same army. Cheap orders, lots of camo markers, brutal close range.
- **Haqqislam.** A neo-Islamic faction that broke away from PanOceania, built around the silk trade, biotech, and the best medicine in the Sphere. Versatile mid-tier infantry, excellent doctors, the Hassassin sub-faction.
- **Nomads.** Three motherships of dissidents, hackers, anarchists, and exiles. The hacker faction. Famously meta-warping (their Bakunin mothership in particular). Aesthetic ranges from grimy techwear to cybernetic body horror.
- **Combined Army.** The alien invasion. Led by the Evolved Intelligence (EI), assimilating species across the galaxy. Big alien monsters, possessed humans, an actual god-killer or two. Heavy hitters with weird tools the human factions don't get.
- **ALEPH.** The Human Sphere's official benevolent superintelligence, fielded as physical avatars and Greek-mythology-themed Recreations. Elite, expensive, mythological flavor (Achilles, Ajax, Hector all on the battlefield).
- **O-12.** The neutral peacekeeping body of the Human Sphere, the UN-equivalent. Newer faction, multi-cultural, designed to feel like a high-tech international intervention force.

Beyond the main eight, each faction has multiple **sectorials**: smaller, more focused armies (PanOceania's Military Orders, Yu Jing's Invincible Army, Combined Army's Morat Aggression Force, etc.) that restrict what units you can field in exchange for unique units, links, and a tighter identity. Most veteran players play sectorials.

The fastest path into N5:

1. **Pick a faction.** Browse the [Corvus Belli store](https://store.corvusbelli.com/infinity) by faction and find an aesthetic you actually like. With Infinity you'll be painting these models a lot, so the look matters as much as the rules.
2. **Get a starter set or Action Pack.** Each faction has a current Action Pack or starter box, about 8–10 models, enough for the first few games. Corvus Belli also runs periodic "Operation:" boxed sets (Crimson Stone, Wildfire, Coldfront, Kaldstrom) that come with two starter forces, dice, terrain, and a learn-to-play book in one box. Those are usually the cheapest entry.
3. **Read the rules for free.** Pull up the [official resources page](https://infinityuniverse.com/resources) and walk through the core mechanics: orders, ARO, the face-to-face roll, and the basic skills. You don't need a hardback.
4. **Build a list on Infinity Army.** [Infinity Army](https://infinityuniverse.com/army/infinity) is the official list builder. Free, browser-based, kept in sync with N5.
5. **Play your first game small.** Most people learn at 150 points first, then push to the standard 300 once orders and ARO feel reflexive. The wiki has "Recon" rules for smaller-format intro games.

## How to get good

Get table time, but also: **play with the wiki open**. Infinity has one of the steeper learning curves in the major skirmish games, and a huge amount of that is just memorizing what each model's skills do and how they interact. Looking things up mid-game is normal even at high level. The wiki is the single best learning tool that exists for the game.

When you want to push past the basics:

- **Watch a live competitive game.** [Goonhammer's Infinity coverage](https://www.goonhammer.com/category/infinity/) is a good starting point for written tactics and battle reports. Seeing how an experienced player sequences orders is worth ten read-throughs of the rules.
- **Play one faction for a long time.** Infinity's depth comes from knowing your sectorial cold: what every unit costs, what every skill does, what the optimal order spend looks like in your most common scenarios. Bouncing between factions slows that down dramatically.
- **Learn the missions.** The competitive game is the ITS mission pack, which changes every season. Most experienced players will tell you that the missions, not the lists, decide who wins. Look up the current ITS season on the [official resources page](https://infinityuniverse.com/resources) and play the missions you'd play at a tournament.

The competitive scene runs on **ITS (Infinity Tournament System)**, a ranked global circuit Corvus Belli runs every season. You can play in person at sanctioned tournaments or online via Tabletop Simulator and JTS leagues. Even if you don't plan to compete, dropping into a local ITS event is one of the fastest ways to find regular opponents.

## The Hobbinomicon take

Infinity is the game I keep coming back to when I want a skirmish where every order matters. I run Combined Army, the alien invasion faction, and it's one of the few full factions I'm holding onto through every other purge. The models are genuinely some of the most beautiful sculpts in the hobby, and the rules reward the kind of player who likes thinking three orders ahead.

The learning curve is real. The first few games you spend more time looking up skills than playing the game. That's fine, that's normal, the wiki is right there. Once orders and ARO click, no other skirmish game I've played has the same feeling of every single decision mattering.

If you've bounced off Infinity before because the rules felt overwhelming, N5 is a genuinely good time to come back. They tightened a lot of the friction that built up across N3 and N4, and the Operation:-series starter boxes make the on-ramp the easiest it has ever been.
